Built to outlast

A quality metal roof commonly lasts 50 years or more — often the last roof a home will ever need, versus two or three shingle replacements in the same span.

FACT: Many metal roofs carry finish warranties up to lifetime.

Storm & fire tough

Standing-seam systems are rated up to 180 mph and are Class A fire-rated. Interlocking panels with concealed fasteners give wind nothing to grab.

MYTH: "Metal attracts lightning." It doesn't — and it won't burn.

Cooler & greener

Reflective factory finishes bounce solar heat before it reaches the attic, trimming cooling bills. And metal is 100% recyclable at end of life.

FACT: Reflective coatings can cut cooling costs by up to 25%.

Quiet & low-upkeep

Over solid decking and underlayment, metal is no louder in the rain than shingles — and there's no granule loss, curling, or re-nailing to manage.

MYTH: "Metal is noisy." That's bare barn roofs, not modern homes.

Common questions about metal roofs

How long does a metal roof really last?
A quality metal roof commonly lasts 50 years or more — two to three times a typical asphalt shingle roof. Many carry finish warranties up to lifetime, which is why it's often the last roof a home ever needs.
Is a metal roof noisy when it rains?
No. Installed over solid decking and underlayment (the way a home roof is built), metal is no louder in a downpour than shingles. The "tin-roof" sound comes from bare metal over open framing, like a barn.
Does metal rust, especially near the coast?
Modern metal roofs resist it. Aluminum is naturally rust-proof and ideal for salt air, and coated Galvalume® steel is engineered against corrosion — both backed by finish warranties. Material choice matters most within a few miles of the water.
Will a metal roof lower my bills or insurance?
Often, yes. Reflective finishes can cut cooling costs by up to 25%, and a wind-rated metal system documented on a wind-mitigation inspection can earn insurance premium credits in Florida.
Does metal attract lightning?
No — metal roofing does not increase the odds of a lightning strike. And because it's non-combustible (Class A fire-rated), if lightning did strike nearby, a metal roof is actually less likely to ignite than many other materials.
